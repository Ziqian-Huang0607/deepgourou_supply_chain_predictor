# Chinese Food Supply Chain Seasonality Research
## For Jan-March to April Forecasting Competition

**Research Date:** 2026-07-07
**Scope:** Food products (pickled vegetables, meat, rice, oil, etc.) in China
**Prediction Target:** April demand based on January-March data

---

## Executive Summary

April in China represents a **critical transition period** in the food supply chain calendar. It sits at the intersection of:
- **Post-Lunar New Year demand depression** (inventory depletion phase)
- **Qingming Festival** (April 4-6, 2026) demand spike for specific foods
- **Spring seasonality shift** toward lighter, fresher foods
- **Solar term transitions** (Qingming -> Guyu) driving traditional consumption patterns
- **Rising temperatures** reducing demand for heavy/staple foods (pork, oil)

**Key Forecasting Insight:** April demand will likely show a **complex mixed pattern** -- depressed baseline demand for staples (meat, oil, rice) due to post-LNY effects and warming weather, BUT **elevated demand for specific categories** (pickled vegetables as spring appetizers, specific Qingming foods, outdoor dining items).

## 2. Qingming Festival Food Demand Impact

### 2.1 Festival Overview

Qingming Festival (also called Tomb Sweeping Day) is one of China's oldest festivals (2,500+ years). It has a **dual nature**:
- **Morning/early day:** Ancestral remembrance (tomb sweeping, food offerings)
- **Afternoon/late day:** Spring celebration (outdoor dining, family meals)

### 2.2 Traditional Qingming Foods with Surging Demand

| Food Item (Chinese) | Description | Demand Surge |
|---------------------|-------------|-------------|
| **青团 (Qingtuan)** | Green glutinous rice balls with sweet bean paste, dyed with mugwort/barley grass | **Major spike** - flagship Qingming food |
| **馓子 (Sanzi)** | Deep-fried crispy dough twists ("cold food") | **Major spike** - traditional cold food |
| **艾粄 (Ai Ban)** | Hakka mugwort rice cake | Moderate in Hakka regions |
| **清明糯米饭** | Glutinous rice with herbs | Moderate |
| **螺蛳 (Freshwater snails)** | Stir-fried snails ("Qingming snails surpass geese") | **Significant spike** in Jiangnan |
| **鸡蛋 (Eggs)** | Boiled eggs (eaten for health all year) | Moderate increase |
| **薄饼/春卷** | Thin pancakes/spring rolls | Moderate |
| **枣糕 (Jujube cake)** | Cold jujube cake | Moderate |

### 2.3 Consumer Behavior Data (2024-2025 Surveys)

From iiMedia Research (艾媒咨询):
- **61.66%** of consumers eat special Qingming festival foods
- **37.06%** choose Qingtuan (most popular)
- **34.58%** choose glutinous rice (清明饭)
- **30.60%** choose Ai Ban (艾糍/艾粄)
- **71.64%** purchase these foods themselves (vs. homemade/gifted)
- Purchase channels: Comprehensive e-commerce (47.92%), bakeries (41.32%), supermarkets (38.89%)

### 2.4 Qingming Festival Restaurant/Catering Impact

**Key demand drivers for restaurant/B2B food supply:**

1. **Tomb Sweeping Food Offerings:** Families bring food offerings to graves (roast pork, dumplings, tea, rice wine, fruit) -- creates demand for specific ingredients
2. **Post-Ceremony Family Meals:** After tomb sweeping, families gather for large meals at restaurants or home
3. **Spring Outing (踏青) Dining:** Massive spike in outdoor dining, picnic foods, portable snacks
4. **"Cold Food" Tradition:** Some regions maintain Cold Food Festival traditions (no hot cooking) -- demand for pre-prepared, cold-consumption items

**2026 Qingming holiday consumption data:**
- Average daily sales of key retail/catering enterprises: **+2.4% YoY**
- Hotel accommodation: **+2.6% YoY**
- Intercity travel trips: **+15.1% YoY**
- Car rental orders: **~+40% YoY**
- Estimated **845.38 million passenger trips** made during the holiday (+6% YoY)

### 2.5 "Spring Outing Economy" (踏青经济)

April is peak season for China's "spring outing economy":
- **"Taqing外卖" (outing takeout)** category booms -- portable foods for outdoor consumption
- Popular items: Smoked fish, chicken feet, steamed buns, pastries, cold dishes
- Cross-provincial travel demand: **63%** of Qingming trips (Meituan data)
- Restaurant operators predict **30%+** increase in foot traffic during Qingming

## 3. Lunar New Year (Spring Festival) Effects

### 3.1 LNY Timing and Pattern (2026)

| Detail | 2026 Date |
|--------|-----------|
| **Spring Festival Holiday** | February 15 - February 23, 2026 |
| **Chinese New Year Day** | February 17, 2026 (Tuesday) |
| **Total Holiday** | 9 days (including make-up workdays) |
| **Pre-LNY Peak Stocking** | Mid-January to early February |
| **Post-LNY Return to Normal** | Late February to early March |

### 3.2 Pre-LNY Stocking Behavior

- **~68%** of Chinese consumers stock up on food/snacks for LNY (FY Ads Singapore data)
- Key stocking categories: meat (especially pork), cooking oil, rice, preserved foods, snacks
- Families fill refrigerators/freezers with LNY provisions
- Restaurant/business demand surges before LNY (company banquets, reunion dinners)

### 3.3 Post-LNY Demand Depression (CRITICAL for April Forecasting)

**This is one of the most important patterns for predicting April:**

| Period | Demand Pattern |
|--------|---------------|
| **Late Feb (post-LNY)** | Sharp demand drop as households consume LNY leftovers |
| **March** | Continued low demand - "seasonal consumption downturn" (季节性消费需求下降) |
| **April** | Gradual recovery but still in low-demand trough for many categories |

**Key data points:**
- National Bureau of Statistics: "Post-holiday consumption demand seasonally declines" (春节后消费需求季节性回落)
- Pork demand specifically: "After Spring Festival, households enter pork inventory digestion period lasting 1-2 months" (春节后1-2个月为猪肉库存消化期)
- March CPI typically drops significantly post-LNY (e.g., -1.0% MoM in March 2024 vs +1.0% in Feb 2024)
- Food prices drop post-LNY: fresh vegetables, pork, eggs, fruits all decline in March

### 3.4 Inventory Depletion Effect

- "Chinese growth just around the corner... inventory adjustments were inevitable through Q1... steep sales growth, rather than inventory depletion, should emerge from April" (NH Investment & Securities analysis)
- By **April**, household LNY inventory depletion is largely complete
- This means **April represents a return to normal purchasing patterns**, not continued depression

## 4. Meat Demand Seasonality (Critical for Forecasting)

### 4.1 Pork Consumption Seasonality

Pork is China's most consumed meat (~63% of meat consumption). The seasonal pattern is extremely well-documented:

| Month | Demand Level | Driver |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| January | Very High | Pre-LNY stocking, banquet demand |
| February | High (early), then crash (post-LNY) | LNY celebration, then inventory digestion |
| March | Low | Post-LNY depression, inventory consumption |
| **April** | **Low (trough)** | **Post-LNY effect + warming weather reducing demand** |
| May | Low-Slight Recovery | Some improvement but still weak |
| June-August | Low | Summer heat suppresses appetite |
| September | Moderate | School starts, slight recovery |
| October | Moderate-High | National Day Golden Week |
| November-January | High | Winter demand, cured meat preparation |

### 4.2 April Pork Demand Specifics

**Multiple sources confirm April is a pork demand trough:**

1. **"After Spring Festival, pork consumption enters seasonal decline phase"** -- National Development and Reform Commission expert
2. **"April pork consumption shows slight decrease, meat share drops 1.02% MoM"** -- Mysteel agricultural products survey
3. **"With weather warming, southern cities enter summer mode early -- people prefer light, low-fat, vitamin-rich foods. Pork has high fat content, so summer dietary demand gradually decreases"** -- Mysteel analysis
4. **"April pork prices show seasonal decline"** -- Zhangjiagang Statistics Bureau (8.3% MoM drop in April 2022)
5. **"Slaughterhouse operating rates maintain low levels post-LNY, currently at 28.44%, down 0.21% WoW, down 3.76% YoY"** -- industry data

### 4.3 Mutton and Beef Seasonality

- Mutton prices: **rise in autumn/winter, fall in spring/summer**
- "Extreme values of rise and fall appear around Spring Festival"
- Beef has strong substitution effect with mutton (transmission elasticity: 0.634)
- Spring (March-May) is generally a **low-demand period** for red meats

### 4.4 Implication for April Forecasting

**Meat demand in April will be depressed** compared to January-March due to:
- Post-LNY inventory depletion continuing from March
- Warmer weather shifting preferences away from heavy meat dishes
- No major meat-intensive holidays in April (unlike LNY or Mid-Autumn)
- Dietary shift toward lighter, vegetable-rich foods

## 6. Spring Seasonality Effects on Food Categories

### 6.1 General Spring Demand Shifts

**From winter (hearty) to spring (light):**

| Category | Winter Pattern | Spring (April) Pattern |
|----------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Cooking Oil** | Heavy use for frying, braising | Moderate - lighter cooking methods |
| **Pork** | High demand (stews, braised) | **Reduced demand** - too fatty for warm weather |
| **Rice** | Steady staple consumption | Steady - slight increase with lighter meat dishes |
| **Pickled Vegetables** | Moderate (winter preservation) | **INCREASE** - appetite stimulant in warm weather |
| **Fresh Vegetables** | Limited variety, expensive | **SURGE** - spring vegetables abundant |
| **Tea** | Moderate | **SURGE** - spring tea harvest season |

### 6.2 Cooking Oil Seasonality

- China is world's largest vegetable oil consumer
- Top oils: Soybean oil (44%), rapeseed oil (24%), palm oil (18%), peanut oil (9%)
- Regional preferences: North (soybean), Central/West (rapeseed), South (peanut)
- Spring demand: **Moderate decline** from winter peak as cooking becomes lighter
- No major oil-intensive festivals in April

### 6.3 Rice Consumption

- Rice is China's staple grain - relatively stable consumption year-round
- Slight seasonal variation: higher consumption in spring/summer when paired with lighter vegetable dishes (instead of heavy meat)
- Chinese per capita rice consumption has been declining slowly but remains substantial
- No major rice-specific demand drivers in April

### 6.4 Pickled Vegetables / Preserved Foods

**This category is particularly important for the competition:**

- Traditional saying: "小雪腌菜，大雪腌肉" (Xiaoxue solar term = pickle vegetables, Daxue = cure meat)
- Pickled vegetables production: seasonal, starting in late autumn (post-harvest)
- **Spring consumption pattern**: Pickled vegetables serve as appetite stimulants when warm weather reduces appetite
- "Dongbei suancai" (Northeast sauerkraut): production starts October, consumption continues through winter into early spring
- By April, warmer temperatures end the traditional fermentation season
- **Retail demand**: Pickled vegetables popular as convenient side dishes for spring outdoor dining
- **Restaurant demand**: Popular appetizer/starter dish, pairs well with lighter spring menus

## 7. Restaurant / B2B Supply Chain Ordering Patterns

### 7.1 Restaurant Procurement Cycle

**Standard industry practice:**
- Most restaurants place orders **weekly or bi-weekly**
- Some place monthly orders
- Ordering typically follows: **Forecast -> Scale recipes -> Subtract inventory**

### 7.2 Weekly Ordering Patterns

While specific China data was limited, general restaurant industry patterns suggest:

| Day | Ordering Behavior |
|-----|------------------|
| **Monday** | Higher orders - restocking after weekend consumption |
| **Tuesday-Wednesday** | Standard replenishment |
| **Thursday-Friday** | Pre-weekend stocking |
| **Saturday-Sunday** | Reduced ordering (restaurants use inventory) |

### 7.3 Chinese Food Service B2B Landscape

Major B2B food supply platforms in China:
- **Meicai (美菜网)** - Major B2B food supply platform
- **Shuhai (蜀海)** - Haidilao's supply chain arm
- **Traditional wholesale markets** - Still dominant for many restaurants

### 7.4 Holiday Impact on B2B Ordering

**Critical pattern for Qingming (April 4-6, 2026):**

| Timing | B2B Ordering Pattern |
|--------|---------------------|
| **April 1-3 (Wed-Fri)** | Pre-holiday stocking surge - restaurants stock up before 3-day closure |
| **April 4-6 (Sat-Mon)** | Holiday - many restaurants closed or reduced service; minimal ordering |
| **April 7-8 (Tue-Wed)** | Post-holiday restocking - moderate surge |
| **April 9-30** | Normal operations |

**Key insight:** B2B orders will show a **pre-holiday spike (April 1-3)** and **post-holiday restock (April 7-8)** pattern surrounding the Qingming holiday.

### 7.5 Chinese Supply Chain Holiday Disruption Pattern

From supply chain research:
- "Orders surge before the breaks. Production slows earlier than expected. Afterward, operations restart unevenly."
- "Buyers know production time will be limited, so demand gets pulled forward"
- "In 2026, Spring Festival in mid-to-late February means January becomes a pressure point"
- For Qingming: Smaller effect than Spring Festival but still causes short-term capacity reduction

### 8.2 Product-Specific Forecasting Guidance

| Product Category | April Demand Signal | Recommended Approach |
|-----------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Pickled Vegetables** | **ELEVATED** - appetite stimulant + outdoor dining | Model with positive Qingming effect, slight spring uplift |
| **Meat (Pork)** | **DEPRESSED** - post-LNY trough + warm weather | Model with negative post-LNY trend, low April baseline |
| **Cooking Oil** | **MODERATE-LOW** - lighter spring cooking | Model with gradual decline from Jan peak |
| **Rice** | **STABLE** - staple with slight increase | Model with stable pattern, slight upward trend |
| **Qingming-specific foods** | **MAJOR SPIKE** | Create separate holiday demand model |

### 8.3 Critical Interaction Effects

1. **Qingming x Product Type:** Only specific products see demand surge (Qingtuan ingredients, Sanzi, eggs, snails, cold dishes)
2. **Post-LNY x Product Type:** Meat/oil most affected; pickled vegetables may be less affected or even benefit
3. **Temperature x Product Type:** April warming differentially affects categories (reduces pork demand, increases vegetable/light food demand)
4. **Weekend x Qingming:** April 4-6 being Sat-Mon means the holiday effectively captures a full weekend, maximizing outdoor dining
